How to Meet People After Moving to a New City in 2026

Moving to a new city is one of the most exciting and isolating experiences in adult life. The excitement of a fresh start is quickly tempered by the reality that you are surrounded by strangers, your social support system is hundreds or thousands of miles away, and the built-in social infrastructure you relied on -- school, college friends, neighborhood familiarity -- no longer exists. Approximately 40 million Americans move each year, and surveys consistently show that social isolation is the number one challenge they face in their new location.

Building a social life from scratch as an adult requires deliberate effort and strategy. Friendships do not form through proximity alone the way they did in college. As an adult, you must actively create opportunities for repeated interaction, show up consistently, and be willing to invest time in relationships before they feel natural. This guide provides actionable strategies for meeting people, building friendships, and creating a fulfilling social life in your new city.

Your First Weeks: Set the Foundation

The first 4-6 weeks in a new city are critical. Your motivation to meet people is at its highest, and your schedule is not yet locked into routines that exclude social opportunities. Use this window strategically. Start by exploring your neighborhood on foot. Visit the local coffee shops, grocery stores, parks, and restaurants. Become a regular at one or two spots -- familiarity breeds connection, and staff and fellow regulars will begin recognizing and greeting you within a few weeks.

Research your city's social landscape before you arrive or immediately after. Every city has its social hubs: the neighborhoods where people gather, the events that draw crowds, the organizations that anchor community life. Search "[your city] events this week" on Google, check Eventbrite and Facebook Events, browse your city's subreddit (r/[cityname]), and look at local alternative newspapers and magazines that list community events. Make a list of 5-10 things to try in your first month and commit to attending at least two social events per week.

The Rule of Three

You need to see someone at least three times in a social context before there is enough familiarity to suggest a one-on-one meetup (coffee, lunch, a walk). This is why consistency matters more than variety. Attending the same weekly running group three times in a row builds more connection than attending three different events once each.

Best Apps for Meeting People in a New City

Technology has created viable pathways for adult friendship that did not exist a decade ago. Bumble BFF is the most popular friendship app, using the same swipe-based matching interface as Bumble's dating app but exclusively for platonic friendships. Users create profiles highlighting their interests and what they are looking for in a friend, and matched pairs can chat and arrange meetups. The app works best in cities with populations over 100,000 and is particularly effective for women aged 22-40.

Meetup is arguably the single most effective tool for meeting people in a new city. Rather than one-on-one matching, Meetup organizes group activities by interest: hiking groups, board game nights, photography walks, tech meetups, language exchange groups, book clubs, cooking classes, and hundreds of other categories. The group format reduces the pressure of one-on-one interaction and lets you meet multiple people in a low-stakes environment. Search for groups in your area, join 3-5 that align with your interests, and attend regularly.

Nextdoor connects you with people in your immediate neighborhood and surfaces local events, recommendations, and community discussions. Facebook Groups remain surprisingly effective for finding niche communities ("New to [City Name]" groups, hobby-specific groups, professional networking groups). Reddit's city-specific subreddits often have monthly meetups and are particularly good for finding people with niche interests. Eventbrite surfaces local events ranging from free community gatherings to paid workshops and classes.

Join Hobby-Based Communities

Shared activities are the most reliable foundation for adult friendships because they provide a natural conversation topic, a regular meeting schedule, and a shared identity. The specific hobby matters less than the regularity of interaction and the social format of the group. Join something that meets at least weekly and involves interaction, not just parallel activity.

High-interaction hobbies include recreational sports leagues (kickball, volleyball, softball, soccer -- most cities have adult recreational leagues through organizations like WAKA, ZogSports, or local parks and recreation departments), group fitness classes (CrossFit gyms are famous for their community culture, but regular yoga studios, running clubs, and cycling groups also build strong social bonds), creative classes (pottery, painting, photography, improv comedy, creative writing workshops), book clubs, board game groups, community theater, cooking classes, and dance classes (salsa, swing, and social dance forms are inherently interactive).

The key is commitment. Attending a pottery class once is a nice experience. Attending the same pottery class every Thursday for three months builds friendships with the people beside you at the wheel. Show up consistently, learn people's names, and gradually extend the interaction beyond the activity itself: "Want to grab coffee after class?" is all it takes to move from activity-mates to friends.

Volunteering: Purpose and Connection

Volunteering combines two powerful elements: contributing to something meaningful and meeting people who share your values. Unlike hobby groups where the common interest might be superficial, volunteer organizations attract people motivated by shared values -- environmental stewardship, food security, animal welfare, education equity -- which creates a foundation for deeper connections.

Find volunteer opportunities through VolunteerMatch.org, your city's volunteer center, local food banks (Feeding America has a zip code search tool), Habitat for Humanity, animal shelters, literacy programs, environmental organizations, and community gardens. The best volunteering for meeting people involves regular, team-based work rather than one-off events: weekly food bank shifts, ongoing mentoring programs, and regular cleanup events where you see the same people each time.

Building Connections Through Work

If you moved for a job, your workplace is a natural starting point for social connections. Accept lunch invitations, attend after-work social events, and join workplace clubs or sports teams if they exist. Be genuinely curious about your colleagues' lives outside of work -- ask about their weekends, hobbies, and neighborhoods. Many of the deepest adult friendships begin as workplace relationships that extend beyond the office.

For remote workers, coworking spaces provide the social interaction that a traditional office offers. Spaces like WeWork, Industrious, and local independent coworking spaces host community events, happy hours, and collaborative projects. Even if you have a home office, working from a coworking space 1-2 days per week exposes you to a community of local professionals. Many cities also have "remote worker meetups" or "digital nomad" groups on Meetup that provide social connection for people who work independently.

Getting to Know Your Neighbors

Your neighbors are the most accessible potential friends because proximity makes repeated interaction effortless. Introduce yourself when you move in -- knock on adjacent doors with a simple introduction. In apartments, common areas like lobbies, laundry rooms, mailboxes, and rooftop terraces are natural interaction points. In neighborhoods with houses, front yards, dog walks, and taking out the trash create regular opportunities for brief conversations that build familiarity over time.

Host a low-key housewarming or "meet the neighbor" gathering within your first month. It does not need to be elaborate -- pizza and drinks in your apartment, or a casual cookout in your yard. The gesture signals openness and gives your neighbors a reason to introduce themselves. Many people in your building or on your street may also be looking for local social connections but waiting for someone else to make the first move.

Maintaining and Deepening New Friendships

Meeting people is the easy part. Turning acquaintances into genuine friends requires follow-through that many adults neglect. After connecting with someone at an event or through an app, follow up within 48 hours with a specific suggestion: "Want to check out that coffee shop on Main Street Saturday morning?" is far more effective than a vague "We should hang out sometime." Specific plans get accepted; vague intentions get forgotten.

Be the organizer. Most people are happy to attend social gatherings but few are willing to plan them. By taking the initiative to organize group outings, dinner parties, or activity meetups, you become the social hub that connects people -- and your new friends will associate you with positive social experiences. Create a group chat with people you have met and regularly suggest activities.

Accept that new friendships feel different from established ones. You will not have the shared history, inside jokes, and effortless comfort that comes from years of friendship. That depth develops over time. Do not abandon new friendships because they do not immediately feel as deep as the ones you left behind. Give them a year of consistent investment before judging their quality.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to make friends after moving?

Research shows approximately 50 hours of interaction for a casual friendship, 90 hours for a genuine friendship, and 200+ hours for a close friendship. Most people feel socially settled after 6-12 months of active effort. Consistency is key -- attend the same groups regularly.

What are the best apps for making friends in a new city?

Bumble BFF for one-on-one friend matching, Meetup for group activities by interest, Nextdoor for neighborhood connections, Facebook Groups for niche communities, and Eventbrite for local events and classes.

How do introverts make friends in a new city?

Join structured activities with a clear focus (classes, book clubs, running groups, volunteering). These provide shared activities that reduce conversation pressure. Start with one-on-one coffee meetups rather than large group events. Online communities with local meetups bridge the comfort gap.

Is it harder to make friends as an adult?

Yes. Adults lack the built-in social infrastructure of school (daily proximity, shared experiences, unstructured social time). Adult friendships require intentional effort: joining groups, attending events, and following up with people. It is harder but absolutely possible with consistent effort.

What should you avoid when trying to make friends?

Avoid waiting for friendships to happen naturally, oversharing too early, putting all efforts into one social circle, comparing new friendships to old ones, and declining invitations because you are not in the mood. Say yes consistently and follow up within 48 hours.
